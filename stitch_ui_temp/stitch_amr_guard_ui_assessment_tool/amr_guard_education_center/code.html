What is AMR

Antimicrobial Resistance occurs when bacteria, viruses, fungi, and parasites change over time and no longer respond to medicines.

1.27M Deaths annually (2019)

SOURCE: CDC

Superbugs

Strains of bacteria that have developed resistance to multiple antibiotics. Examples include MRSA and carbapenem-resistant Enterobacterales.

2.8M US infections per year
health_and_safety
SOURCE: NHS

Prevention

Simple actions like hand hygiene, completing prescribed doses, and only using antibiotics when necessary can drastically slow the spread.

Knowledge Check

Myth vs Fact

Debunking common misconceptions about antibiotic usage.

MYTH "Antibiotics work against viral infections like the cold or flu."
expand_more

The Truth: Antibiotics are only effective against bacterial infections. Taking them for viruses doesn't cure the illness and contributes to resistance.

Learn more about viral pathways
FACT "Even if you feel better, you must finish the entire course."
expand_more

The Truth: Stopping early allows the strongest bacteria to survive and multiply, potentially leading to a more severe, resistant infection.

CDC Dosing Guidelines
MYTH "AMR is only a problem for people who take antibiotics frequently."
expand_more

The Truth: Resistance happens to the bacteria, not the person. Anyone can contract an infection caused by a resistant superbug, regardless of their medical history.
